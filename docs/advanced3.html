Prefixes

One characteristic feature of Old English verbs is the extensive use of prefixes added to a base verb in order to change its meaning. If this sounds strange, remember that modern English does this as well. We have verbs like 'to undo', which means the opposite of 'to do' - the prefix un- has altered the meaning of the verb - in fact, it has completely reversed the meaning. This is the same for many verbs ('unlock', 'uncouple', 'unfasten', and so on), and it is certainly not the only prefix. Look at the table below: you should be able to work out what the common meaning of the prefix is in each case.

Prefixes in Modern English
Prefix Examples
re- reappear, revisit, restructure
mis- mislead, misapply, misinterpret
over- overeat, oversleep, overbake
dis- discontinue, disinter, disappear
un- undo, unpack, untie, unfriend

Some of these words are inherited from Old English, and some of them are more recent coinages. Because the prefix has a set meaning (such as under- meaning 'not enough' or 'beneath'), new verbs can be created. There was no 'underselling' in early medieval England - or at least, not by that name - but the word was coined from two Old English roots: the verb sellan and the preposition under. In fact, many words were prefixed with under- in Old English, including underwritan, 'undergangan - to undergo', and 'underberan - to support (literally, 'to bear beneath')'.

In Old English there are some regularly used prefixes that are recognisable from their modern day descendants, but there are also some that are less familiar. For example, we might recognise the term forberan (meaning forbear, or endure), but Old English also has 'geberan - to bring forth/support ', 'aberan - to endure or to bear away', and 'forþberan - to bear forth'. Below is a table of some of the most common verbal prefixes and their meanings.

Prefixes in Old English
Prefix Meaning Examples
be- Around, about began - surround, bescitan - to shit all over
for- Intensifier or 'away from' forbrecan - break up, forstelan - steal away
ge- Indicating completeness/perfection gebringan - to bring forth, geascian - inquire
on- Reverse, or up/on onlucan - unlock, onwacan - wake up
to- Break apart todǣlan - divide up, tosendan - to disperse
ut- Out utgang - exit, utdragan - drag/draw out
ymb- Around, about ymbsittan - sit around, ymbþencan - to think about

As well as modifying verbs, prefixes are often added to nouns with a smiliar effect. So, the noun un-weder means 'bad weather' and the noun utscytan (from scyt - defacate + suffix 'ut') gives us a very descriptive noun for diarrhoea! One thing to bear in mind with the prefix ge-, which you've already come across in the past participles section of this module, is that it has a similar 'perfective' effect when placed in front of the participle - in the sense that it indicates a completed action - so the past participle of 'slean - to slay' may be written as slegen - heard, or as geslegen, with the prefix ge- reinforcing its completedness.
