Strong Feminine Nouns

Feminine nouns have significant differences both in how they decline and the demonstratrive pronouns they use compared to Strong Masculine nouns, but within their own category, the cases are very similar to each other. For example, the accusative, genitive and dative singular endings for Strong Feminine Nouns is e, and the nominative, accusative, and genitive plural endings are generally a.

Much like neuter nouns, feminine nouns only take -u in the nominative singular if they have a short syllable, otherwise they take no suffix. So the singular nominative of words like giefu - gift, lufu - love and sceadu - shadow keep the 'u' as they are short-syllable words, but cwen - queen, wund - wound and ides - woman have no 'u' as they are long-syllable words. Examine the two sentences below: 'The queen went to the city' and 'The gift sent to the woman'.

Words with two syllables will often lose the vowel of the second syllable when it gains a suffix. This makes it easier to pronounce. This is why the 'e' and the 'r' seem to swap positions in the singular accusative, genitive and dative of ceaster - city. It loses the 'e' in all conjugations where it gains a suffix. You will see the same vowel-drop in sawol - soul
